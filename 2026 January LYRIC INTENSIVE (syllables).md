2. Response to a question \#1: What about Japanese poetry? 

**Choka**–”**long poem**” Earliest Japanese poem style. Unrhymed. Written in five and seven syllable lines, ending on an “extra” seven syllable line. There was often an “envoy” (or “envoi”) at the end, giving thanks to or praising a patron of the poet, or some other important person or event. The oldest “choka” that we know about is from the 1st century. A battle poem…like the song Brian wrote\! In Japanese most syllables are the SAME stress, so syllables themselves, how many and how long, matter a lot.

**Hakai de Renga** (or just “**renga**”)--”**linked poem**” Hakai literally means “playful style,” and it’s a group poem, like the one you wrote while I was in Japan. Poets composed lines of 5 syllables, 7 syllables, 5 syllables, 7 syllables, 7 syllables by taking turns composing alternate three line and two line groups. Yep. This is where you see what became “Haiku.” This five line stanza is a “renga” which became part of a longer poem. That type of stanza also became a poem style of its own called “tanka.”

4. Lesson: Syllables–**January Lyric Focus**  
   

Syllables are rhythmic units in words. In lyric writing we use sustained syllables, one beat syllables, and silence along with other musical beats. In Dolly Parton’s “Jolene,” for example, that two syllable name sometimes takes a beat plus a beat and a half (♪|♩.), sometimes four beats (♩♩), sometimes six and a half beats (♬♬♩♩). Here I’m using “beat” to indicate eight rhythmic units withing a 4/4 meter. Those are the first three times that Parton sings the two syllable name, Jolene. She also uses a full beat of SILENCE before following the name with mostly single beats for “I’m begging of you please don’t take my man.” Every beat counts–especially the silence.

One of the (many) reasons Japanese forms of lyricism help English speakers identify powerful syllables is that we suddenly hear the silence that surrounds each sound. In Japanese the effect of the 5/7/5 syllable form is VERY DIFFERENT than in English because every vowel goes with a syllable\! There are no diphthongs in Japanese. In English many of the names of vowels are diphthongs: a, i, o, u….

Last week I misspoke when I said that English has “fewer” words than Japanese…but the meanings of English words usually have fewer layers. As artists we use many of the layers of meaning to produce our work.

Bonus: Renga Chain  
Start a tanka conversation, where two or more people exchange complete tankas, each written in response to a received tanka. Another option is to break the tanka into the upper part (5/7/5) and lower part (7/7), with different poets composing the different parts of the same tanka.
